How a budgeting engagement runs

Four steps from numbers to decisions.

Starting point

Current position

We review your existing accounts, cash position and any prior budgets so the new plan starts from reality, not a blank page.

Build

Forecast built

Income, costs and timing are modelled month by month, with scenarios for the decisions you're actually weighing up.

Check-in

Reviewed quarterly

Actuals are checked against the plan each quarter, so drift gets caught early instead of at year end.

Adjust

Plan updated

The forecast is revised as circumstances change, keeping it a live decision-making tool rather than a filed document.

Common questions

Budgeting, answered plainly

The questions we're asked most before a first budgeting conversation.

A budget sets planned income and spending targets for a period, usually a year. A cash flow forecast tracks the timing of money actually moving in and out of the business, so you can see whether you'll have enough cash on hand even in a profitable month.

Most businesses benefit from a full annual budget with a quarterly review against actuals. Businesses in a growth phase, or with seasonal cash flow, often review monthly instead, since conditions change faster than an annual cycle can capture.

Yes. A budget built around growth scenarios shows what investment, hiring or borrowing a business can actually afford at each stage, turning growth from a hope into a funded plan.

Capital budgeting evaluates long-term investment decisions, such as buying equipment, property or another business, by weighing the upfront cost against expected returns over time. It's used whenever a decision will still be affecting the business years from now.
